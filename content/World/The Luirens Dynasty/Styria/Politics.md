Styrian politics are defined by two forces. Tradition stands at its core, shaped by ancient customs and long standing obligations. Alongside this lies the influence of the Crownlands, inherited when Styria became their subject.

The system is an aristocracy. Power rests with noble houses who own the land, command armies, and collect taxes in the name of their authority.
***
# Tradition

Oaths occupy a central place in Styrian tradition and are binding to the highest degree. Knights swear oaths to nobles, nobles to other nobles, and so the chain continues. Historical records claim this custom originates in ancient times, when blood oaths were sworn to bind two fates together. Though such rituals are now only spoken, their weight and significance endure.

In the line of succession, the eldest child inherits all titles. This tradition is deeply respected, and as a result Styria has not seen a succession war for centuries. Even so, heirs often share land and wealth with their siblings, preserving noble bloodlines while maintaining a centralized hold on power. This custom also traces its origins to ancient times, when Styria was ruled by warring tribes. With lifespans shortened by constant conflict, the individual deemed second most fit to rule within a family was often the eldest child.
***
# Crownlands Influence

Styria was not always united, at least not until the Crownlands's invasion. Relations and alliances had already begun to form, but Styria was not a single state in the way the Crownlands were. This foreign threat and the long lasting war that followed forged that unity. It has endured to the present day, with Styria consistently presenting a united front in its dealings with the Dynasty.

Many aspects of the bureaucracy and nobility were adopted from the Crownlands. Some argue this adoption was forced, and as a result much of Styrian political life reflects that of the Crownlands's.
