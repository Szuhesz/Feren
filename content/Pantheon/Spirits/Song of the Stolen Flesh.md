You stumble upon a Songstone, nearly swallowed by creeping vines and dense vegetation. As you clear away the overgrowth, a long-forgotten tale begins to emerge. A tale lost to those who strayed too far. It speaks in whispers, in echoes of sorrow...

There was once a man of great ambition, one who wished to honour the Great Aspects beyond all others. Yet, no matter how he tried, no matter how he bled for them, the silence grew. His questions found no answers, his steps no longer guided. Fear took root in his heart, for to be severed from the Great Aspects was death in all but breath.

And so, in his despair, he listened when a voice called to him. A Spirit, veiled in mist and knowing. It promised him a path, a way to be seen once more. The man followed, for what choice did he have? But the Spirit was not what it seemed. It was no guide, no guardian. It was a Revenant, a thing of hunger and lies.

“If you wish to be seen,” it whispered, “to be heard, to be one with them… why not become them?”

The man did not understand, not yet, but the thought burrowed deep. So he hunted. One by one, he struck down the Great Aspects’ manifestations. Predator and prey alike, none escaped his wrath. And when the grove called him back, where the Revenant lay waiting, he brought the broken bodies with him.

"Fear not, child," the Revenant murmured, its voice like dead leaves in the wind. "Join them. Become what they would not grant you."

And at last, he understood.

First, the limbs. Then the wings. He wove them into himself, tore flesh and fastened sinew, embedding, replacing, becoming. When all was done, he placed a bear’s head upon his own, a crown of stolen divinity.

"Can you feel it now?" the Revenant purred, wicked delight twisting its voice.

The man smiled. Then he screamed.

The power did not course through him, it devoured. The limbs he took were never his to claim, the spirits within them never meant to share a single vessel. They awoke in fury, in hunger, in wrath. Teeth and claws turned inward. Flesh was sundered, bone split, sinew unmade.

And when the wailing ceased, when the earth had drunk its fill, nothing remained but scattered, bloody offerings. Pieces of many, yet belonging to none.

The Songstone bears images carved in ancient runes, their meaning nearly lost to time. Though much has faded, the final etching remains eerily intact. A grotesque vision preserved in unnatural clarity.

A man, if he could still be called that, stands twisted and wrong. A bear’s head sits upon his shoulders, its empty sockets gaping. His legs are a grotesque fusion, one the sinewy limbs of a wolf, the other the slender, fractured grace of a deer. Wings, tattered and unnaturally stretched, jut from his back, their feathers slick with something dark. His body is draped in a patchwork of fur and hide, stitched together like a thing that should not be.

Even in stone, he seems to move, to breathe, as though the horror of his form refuses to be forgotten.
